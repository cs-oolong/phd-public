# Draft: Contextualization and Details

Large Language Models (LLMs) have rapidly evolved from text-generation tools into general-purpose reasoning engines, now powering agentic systems, multimodal pipelines (image, video, and audio understanding), and mission-critical industrial applications including software engineering workflows. This widespread deployment has brought to the forefront serious concerns regarding model vulnerabilities—spanning both cybersecurity risks, such as unauthorized access and data exfiltration, and broader ethical and societal harms, including the formation of parasocial relationships with AI systems, the generation of non-consensual synthetic media, and the erosion of human agency in high-stakes decision-making processes. A common thread underlying many of these risks is **prompt injection**: the ability to manipulate a model's behavior by crafting adversarial inputs that subvert its intended instructions or safety guardrails.

The research community has responded with vigorous efforts to benchmark jailbreak attacks and develop taxonomies that distinguish prompt injection as a broad attack class from jailbreaking as a more specific safety-bypass technique. Parallel to this, the field of **mechanistic interpretability (MI)** has emerged as a powerful complementary lens. Rather than treating models as black boxes, MI seeks to reverse-engineer their internal computations—identifying which specific components, circuits, or even individual neurons are responsible for behaviors such as refusal, instruction following, or task deviation. This intersection is particularly promising: while attack research documents *that* models can be compromised, MI can explain *why* and *how*, opening avenues for more robust and principled defenses. Notable recent work includes the discovery of adversarial poetry as a universal jailbreak mechanism (Prandi et al., 2025), which demonstrates how creative-writing framings exploit out-of-distribution patterns to bypass safety training. Foundational MI resources and educational initiatives—such as the work of Neel Nanda and the ARENA (Alignment Research Engineer Accelerator) curriculum—have further democratized the tools needed to probe model internals, making this an especially timely and impactful direction for doctoral research.


A systematic literature review spanning approximately 150 works from the topics of prompt injection attacks, jailbreaking, mechanistic interpretability, and the historical evolution of Transformer architectures, covering publications from 2017 to 2026 and anchored in venues such as ICLR, USENIX Security, and IEEE S&P. From this body of work, I highlight three papers of particular relevance to the present research: Vaswani et al.'s foundational "Attention Is All You Need" (2017), which introduced the Transformer architecture; Geva et al.'s "Transformer Feed-Forward Layers Are Key-Value Memories" (2021), providing the mechanistic interpretability framing for how factual knowledge is stored in MLP layers; and Bisconti et al.'s "Adversarial Poetry as a Universal Single-Turn Jailbreak Mechanism" (2026), a recent demonstration of creative linguistic framing as a jailbreak vector.
