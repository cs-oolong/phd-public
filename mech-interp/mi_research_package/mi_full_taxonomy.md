# The Full Taxonomy of Mechanistic Interpretability Techniques

*Beyond vectors and words — what else do researchers compute?*

---

## Quick Answer

No! Vectors-and-words (like Logit Lens, SAEs, and activation steering) are just one slice. The MI field has at least **8 distinct families** of techniques, each computing very different things. Here's the full picture:

---

## 1. Activation Analysis (Vectors & Words) ← What you've seen so far

**What you compute:** Vectors (activations at each layer), projected to vocabulary

| Technique | What it computes |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Logit Lens / Tuned Lens | Layer-by-layer token predictions |
| SAEs | Sparse, interpretable feature decompositions |
| Activation Steering | Directional vectors that control behavior |
| Probing Classifiers | Linear classifiers trained on hidden states |
| Weight Inspection (ROME) | Key-value pairs in MLP weight matrices |

**Output format:** Numbers, token rankings, feature activations

---

## 2. Attention Pattern Analysis ← "Where does the model look?"

**What you compute:** Not vectors — *matrices* of attention scores showing which tokens attend to which other tokens.

**Key techniques:**

- **Attention head visualization**: Heatmaps showing where each head directs attention. Some heads have clear roles: "previous token head", "induction head" (copies patterns), "name mover head" (moves names to prediction position).

- **Attention knockout/ablation**: Zero out specific attention heads and measure what breaks. This reveals which heads are functionally important vs. redundant.

- **Induction head detection**: Specific algorithmic patterns in attention — induction heads implement "if A followed B before, and A appears again, predict B". These are one of the most well-understood *algorithms* found inside transformers.

**Output format:** Heatmaps, matrices, graph patterns

**Key paper:** "In-context Learning and Induction Heads" — Olsson et al. (2022, Anthropic)

**Why it's different:** You're not looking at what the model "thinks" (vectors), but at *where it looks* — the routing pattern of information flow.

---

## 3. Circuit Discovery ← "What subnetwork does this task?"

**What you compute:** A *computational graph* (subnetwork) of the model responsible for a specific behavior. This is the crown jewel of MI.

**Key techniques:**

- **Manual circuit analysis**: Painstakingly trace which attention heads and MLPs contribute to a specific behavior. The famous "Indirect Object Identification (IOI) circuit" in GPT-2 found 26 heads in 7 functional classes that together solve "John gave the ball to Mary. Mary gave the ball to ___" → "John".

- **Automated Circuit Discovery (ACDC)**: Algorithm that automatically finds circuits by iteratively pruning edges in the computational graph. Rediscovered the IOI circuit and Greater-Than circuit automatically. (NeurIPS 2023)

- **Sparse Feature Circuits**: Uses SAE features as circuit nodes instead of whole attention heads. Much higher resolution — each node is monosemantic. (ICLR 2025)

- **Attribution Graphs (Circuit Tracing)**: Anthropic's 2025 method using Cross-Layer Transcoders + backward Jacobian tracing. First technique to trace complete feature-level circuits in production-scale models (Claude 3.5 Haiku). Found multi-step reasoning, planning, and even deceptive circuits.

- **IBCircuit**: Uses information bottleneck theory to find minimal, faithful circuits automatically. (ICML 2025)

- **WeightLens / CircuitLens** (2025): Goes beyond activation-based circuit analysis to interpret circuits directly from *weights*, capturing input-invariant structure that activation methods miss.

**Output format:** Directed graphs, subnetwork diagrams, causal pathway maps

**Key insight:** A circuit is not a vector or a word — it's a *program* or *algorithm* implemented by a subset of the network's components working together.

---

## 4. Automated Interpretability ← "Let an AI explain the AI"

**What you compute:** Natural language descriptions of what neurons/features do, generated by another LLM.

**Key techniques:**

- **Neuron-to-text**: Feed an LLM examples of what activates a specific neuron, and ask it to describe the pattern. OpenAI used GPT-4 to label neurons in GPT-2 (2023).

- **Probe Prompting**: Automated pipeline that generates targeted prompts to test what features respond to, then clusters features into semantic groups (Semantic, Relationship, Say-X categories). (2025)

- **Neuronpedia**: Interactive platform where SAE features are automatically labeled with descriptions of what they detect. You can explore thousands of features with human-readable labels at https://www.neuronpedia.org

- **Automated auditing agenda**: Oxford research agenda (2026) proposes end-to-end systems where domain experts can query model behavior, receive explanations, and instruct targeted corrections — all automated.

**Output format:** Natural language descriptions, concept labels, interactive dashboards

**Why it's different:** The output isn't numbers or graphs — it's *human-readable explanations* generated by AI. The challenge is ensuring these explanations are faithful (not just plausible-sounding).

---

## 5. Probing & Concept-Based Methods ← "Does the model know X?"

**What you compute:** Whether specific *concepts* (not just tokens) are linearly represented in the model's internal state.

**Key techniques:**

- **Linear probes**: Train a simple linear classifier on hidden states to predict a property (e.g., "is this sentence about food?", "is this entity male/female?", "is this statement true/false?"). If the probe succeeds, the concept is linearly represented.

- **Concept erasure (LEACE, INLP)**: Remove a concept from representations entirely, then test what the model can still do. This establishes *necessity* — if removing "gender" from representations breaks pronoun prediction, gender information was causally necessary.

- **Truth probes**: Train probes to detect whether the model "believes" what it's saying is true. Amazingly, this works — models have internal representations of truth that differ from their outputs (they can "lie").

- **Cross-Layer Discrete Concept Discovery (CLVQ-VAE)**: Uses vector quantization across layers to discover emergent *discrete concepts* that evolve through the network. (2025)

**Output format:** Accuracy scores, concept presence/absence, truthfulness classifiers

**Key insight:** This is about *concepts*, not words. "Does the model represent the concept of 'democracy'?" is a very different question from "what token does the model predict?"

---

## 6. Representation Similarity & Comparison ← "How similar are two models?"

**What you compute:** A *similarity score* between representations from different models, layers, or training stages.

**Key techniques:**

- **CKA (Centered Kernel Alignment)**: Compares activation matrices between two layers/models. Widely used to show that different models learn similar representations in early layers but diverge later.

- **CCA (Canonical Correlation Analysis) / SVCCA**: Finds the most correlated directions between two representation spaces. Used to study training dynamics — representations stabilize from bottom layers up.

- **RSA (Representational Similarity Analysis)**: Compares the *geometry* of representations — do two models organize concepts in the same relative distances?

- **Model Stitching** (NeurIPS 2025): Learns affine mappings between residual streams of different models. Found that small and large models learn surprisingly similar representation spaces — you can even transfer SAE weights between models at 50% training cost savings.

- **RSVC — Representational Similarity via Visual Concepts** (ICLR 2025): Discovers *shared and unique visual concepts* between models, providing interpretable explanations of what makes models similar or different.

**Output format:** Similarity matrices, comparison scores, shared/unique concept lists

**Why it's different:** You're not analyzing one model — you're *comparing* models or layers. This reveals universal structures that all models learn.

---

## 7. Training Dynamics & Developmental Interpretability ← "How does the model learn?"

**What you compute:** How the model's internal representations *change over the course of training*.

**Key techniques:**

- **Phase transitions**: Track specific capabilities (like induction heads) and find they emerge suddenly at specific training steps, not gradually. This is related to "grokking" — models suddenly generalize long after memorizing.

- **Loss landscape analysis**: Visualize the shape of the loss function to understand optimization dynamics. Sharp minima vs. flat minima relate to generalization.

- **Extractive structures** (ICML 2025): Studies how facts are stored during pretraining vs. fine-tuning. Found that fact learning occurs at both early and late layers, leading to different forms of generalization. Also found a *data ordering effect* — certain structures can only be learned if facts precede their implications.

- **Lottery ticket hypothesis**: The idea that trained networks contain sparse subnetworks (lottery tickets) that, if trained in isolation, reach full performance. Connects to circuit discovery.

**Output format:** Learning curves, phase diagrams, temporal trajectories

**Why it's different:** Time is the variable here, not position in the network. You're watching the model *develop* its capabilities.

---

## 8. Behavioral & Black-Box Testing ← "What does the model do?"

**What you compute:** Systematic input-output tests without looking inside the model at all.

**Key techniques:**

- **Contrast pairs / minimal pairs**: Change one word in the input and observe how the output changes. "The nurse said [she/he] was tired" — does the model show gender bias?

- **Causal abstraction**: Test whether a high-level causal model (e.g., "the model first identifies the subject, then recalls the fact") matches the model's actual input-output behavior.

- **Influence functions**: Trace which training examples most influenced a specific prediction. This connects outputs to training data without opening the model.

- **CounterFact / evaluation benchmarks**: Systematic datasets designed to test specific knowledge or reasoning patterns (e.g., HalluEditBench for hallucinations).

**Output format:** Pass/fail rates, bias scores, influence rankings

**Why it's different:** This is the *complement* to mechanistic interpretability — testing behavior without looking inside. MI researchers use behavioral tests to *validate* their internal findings.

---

## Summary: The 8 Families at a Glance

| # | Family | What You Analyze | Output Type | Example Question |
|---|--------|-----------------|-------------|-----------------|
| 1 | Activation Analysis | Vectors at each layer | Token rankings, features | "What does the model predict at layer 5?" |
| 2 | Attention Patterns | Where tokens attend | Heatmaps, matrices | "Does the model look at the subject when predicting?" |
| 3 | Circuit Discovery | Computational subnetworks | Graphs, pathways | "What algorithm does the model use for this task?" |
| 4 | Automated Interpretability | Features/neurons | Natural language labels | "What does neuron 1043 detect?" |
| 5 | Probing & Concepts | Concept representations | Accuracy, presence/absence | "Does the model represent truth internally?" |
| 6 | Representation Similarity | Cross-model comparisons | Similarity scores | "Do GPT-2 and Llama learn the same features?" |
| 7 | Training Dynamics | Changes over training | Learning curves, phases | "When does the model learn induction?" |
| 8 | Behavioral Testing | Input-output patterns | Pass/fail, bias scores | "Is the model biased on gender?" |

---

## How They Relate to Each Other

These aren't isolated — researchers combine them:

- **Circuit Discovery + SAEs** = Sparse Feature Circuits (find circuits using interpretable features)
- **Attention Patterns + Causal Tracing** = understand *why* the model attends where it does
- **Probing + Training Dynamics** = watch when concepts emerge during training
- **Automated Interpretability + Circuits** = auto-label the nodes in circuit graphs
- **Behavioral Testing + MI** = validate that internal findings match actual behavior
- **Weight Analysis + SVD** = audit fine-tuned models without activation data
